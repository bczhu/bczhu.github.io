Lie derivatives and Covariant derivatives

周二 10 十一月 2015

In differential geometry, two concepts are always quite difficult to distinguish, one is the Lie derivatives, the other is Covariant derivatives. In order to distinguish them, first, we give the definition of them.

All derivatives want to characterize the aspects of different manifolds, and to compare the quantities between different points in a manifold, we have to set up a rule to map them into each other, different derivatives use different schema for comparison.

The most simple one is direct derivatives defined by \(\partial_{\nu}X^{\mu}\), but in a manifold instead of Euclidean spaces, this quantity has no meaning because different points can have a very different local coordinates (or say different chart (U,\(\phi\))).

So a natural extension is to use mapping to diffeomorphic two different points.

Lie derivatives

The main ingredient of Lie derivatives is the flow and the mapping defined by the flow.

Two basic concepts:

  • Integral curve Let \(X\) be a vector field in \(M\), an integral cruve \(x(t)\) is a curve in \(M\), whose tangent vector at \(x(t)\) is \(X|_x\). Given a chart \((U,\phi)\), this means

    $$\frac{dx^{\mu}}{dt}=X^{\mu}(x(t))$$

  • Flow A flow \(\sigma(t,x)\) is a diffeomorphism from \(M\) to \(M\), denoted by \(\sigma_t:M\to M\), which itself is an one-parameter group of transformations. Under the infinitesimal transformation \(\varepsilon\), we have

    $$\sigma_{\varepsilon}^{\mu}(x)=\sigma^{\mu}(\varepsilon,x)=x^{\mu}+\varepsilon X^{\mu}(x)$$
    The vector field \(X\) is called the infinitesimal generator of the transformation \(\sigma_t\).

Lie derivatives

Let \(\sigma(t,x)\) and \(\tau(t,x)\) be two flows generated by the vector fields \(X\) and \(Y\),

$$\frac{d\sigma^{\mu}(s,x)}{ds}=X^{\mu}(\sigma(s,x))\\ \frac{d\tau^{\mu}(t,x)}{dt}=Y^{\mu}(\tau(t,x))$$

Let us evaluate the change of the vector field \(Y\) along \(\sigma(s,x)\). The Lie derivative of a vector \(Y\) along the flow \(\sigma\) of \(X\) is defined by

$$\mathcal{L}_XY=\lim_{\varepsilon\to 0}\frac{1}{\varepsilon}[(\sigma_{-\varepsilon})_{}Y|_{\sigma_{\varepsilon}(x)}-Y|_x]$$

First we have

\begin{align} Y|_{\sigma_{\varepsilon}(x)}=&Y^{\mu}(x^{\nu}+\varepsilon X^{\nu}(x))e_{\mu}|_{x+\varepsilon X}\\ \approx& Y^{\mu}(x)+\varepsilon X^{\nu}(x)\partial_{\nu}Y^{\mu}(x)e_{\mu}|_{x+\varepsilon X} \end{align}

Then

\begin{align} {\sigma_{-\varepsilon}}_{}Y|_{\sigma_{\varepsilon}(x)}=&(Y^{\mu}(x)+\varepsilon X^{\lambda}(x)\partial_{\lambda}Y^{\mu}(x))\partial\frac{x^{\nu}-\varepsilon X^{\nu}}{\partial x^{\mu}}e_{\nu}|_{x}\\ \approx& (Y^{\mu}+\varepsilon X^{\nu}\partial_{\nu}Y^{\mu}-\varepsilon Y^{\nu}\partial_{\nu}X^{\mu})e_{\mu}|_{x} \end{align}

We get

$$\mathcal{L}_{X}Y=X^{\nu}\partial_{\nu}Y^{\mu}-Y^{\nu}\partial_{\nu}X^{\mu}=[X,Y]$$

For the same routine, we get:

$$\mathcal{L}_{X}\omega=(X^{\nu}\partial_{\nu}\omega_{\mu}+\omega_{\nu}\partial_{\mu}X^{\nu})dx^{\mu}$$

Other derivations

  1. From \(x^{\mu}\to x^{\mu}+\epsilon X^{\mu}\), we have

    \begin{align} Y'^{\mu}(x')=&\frac{\partial x'^{\mu}}{\partial x^{\nu}}Y^{\nu}(x)\\ =&Y^{\mu}(x)+\epsilon \partial_{\nu}X^{\mu}Y^{\nu}(x) \end{align}
    Then we have
    \begin{align} Y'^{\mu}(x)=&Y^{\mu}(x-\epsilon X)+\epsilon \partial_{\nu}X^{\mu}Y^{\nu}(x-\epsilon X)\\ \approx & Y^{\mu}(x)-\epsilon X^{\nu}\partial_{\nu}Y^{\mu}+\epsilon \partial_{\nu}X^{\mu}Y^{\nu}(x) \end{align}
    Then
    $$\mathcal{L}_XY(x)=\lim_{\epsilon\to 0}\frac{Y^{\mu}(x)-Y'^{\mu}(x)}{\epsilon}=X^{\nu}\partial_{\nu}Y^{\mu}-Y^{\nu}\partial_{\nu}X^{\mu}$$

  2. Or from \(Y^{'\mu}(x')=\frac{\partial{x^{'\mu}}}{\partial x^{\nu}}Y^{\nu}(x)\), then we exchange the coordinates \(x \leftrightarrow x'\), we have

    \begin{align} Y^{'\mu}(x)=&\frac{\partial{x^{\mu}}}{\partial x^{'\nu}}Y^{\nu}(x')\\ =&(\delta_{\nu}^{\mu}-\epsilon\partial_{\nu}X^{\mu})(Y^{\nu}+\epsilon X^{\lambda}\partial_{\lambda}Y^{\nu})\\ =&Y^{\mu}+\epsilon X^{\nu}\partial_{\nu}Y^{\mu}-\epsilon Y^{\nu}\partial_{\nu}X^{\mu} \end{align}
    Also gives
    $$\mathcal{L}_XY(x)=\lim_{\epsilon\to 0}\frac{Y'^{\mu}(x)-Y^{\mu}(x)}{\epsilon}=X^{\nu}\partial_{\nu}Y^{\mu}-Y^{\nu}\partial_{\nu}X^{\mu}$$

Meaning of Lie derivative

Geometrically, the Lie bracket shows the non-commutativity of two flows. For two flows \(\sigma(\epsilon,x)\) and \(\tau(\delta,y)\) generate by \(\bf{X}\) and \(\bf{Y}\), we have:

\begin{align} \tau^{\mu}(\delta,\sigma(\epsilon,x)) &\approx \tau^{\mu}(\delta,x^{\nu}+\epsilon X^{\nu}(x))\\ &\approx (x^{\mu}+\epsilon X^{\mu}(x))+\delta Y^{\mu}(x^{\nu}+\epsilon X^{\nu}(x))\\ &\approx x^{\mu}+\epsilon X^{\mu}(x)+\delta Y^{\mu}(x)+\epsilon\delta X^{\nu}(x)\partial_{\nu}Y^{\mu}(x) \end{align}

And

$$\sigma^{\mu}(\epsilon,\tau(\delta,x)) \approx x^{\mu}+\epsilon X^{\mu}(x)+\delta Y^{\mu}(x)+\epsilon\delta Y^{\nu}(x)\partial_{\nu}X^{\mu}(x)$$

Then we get

$$\tau^{\mu}(\delta,\sigma(\epsilon,x))-\sigma^{\mu}(\epsilon,\tau(\delta,x))=\epsilon\delta[X,Y]^{\mu}$$
