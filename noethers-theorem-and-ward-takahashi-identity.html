Noether's theorem and Ward-Takahashi identity

周日 01 十一月 2015

In classical and quantum field theory, it is claimed that every continuous symmetry leads to a conservation law. But the derivation usually takes different form and quite confusing sometimes. Here, we deduce it in different scenarios and also in both classical field and quantum field theory.

1. In the passive point of view

This means the coordinates \(x\) and \(x'\) correspond to the same point but given by different frame, also the integral area \(\Omega\) and \(\Omega'\).

Total derivative: here, we first clarify a very useful concept total derivative. For a general transformation:

$$x_{\mu}\to x'_{\mu}=x_{\mu}+\delta x_{\mu}\\ \phi_r(x)\to\phi_r'(x')=\phi_r(x)+\delta\phi_r(x)$$

Then the total derivative defines by:

\begin{align} \tilde{\delta}\phi_r(x)&\equiv\phi_r'(x)-\phi_r(x)=\phi_r'(x')-\phi_r(x)+\phi_r'(x)-\phi_r'(x')\\ &={\delta}\phi_r(x)-\delta x_{\mu}\partial^{\mu}\phi_r(x) \end{align}

The total derivative has very good property:

$$\partial_{\mu}\tilde{\delta}\phi_r(x)=\tilde{\delta}\partial_{\mu}\phi_r(x)$$

Notice: this total derivative is regardless of whether we are in passive or active viewpoint. Then:

$$\delta S=\int_{\Omega'}d^4x'\mathcal{L}'(x')-\int_{\Omega}d^4x\mathcal{L}(x)$$

where \(\mathcal{L}'(x')\equiv \mathcal{L}(\phi'(x'),\partial'_{\mu}\phi'(x'),x')\). From \(\mathcal{L}'(x')=\mathcal{L}(x)+\delta\mathcal{L}(x)\), which gives

$$\delta S=\int_{\Omega'}d^4x'\delta\mathcal{L}(x)+\int_{\Omega'}d^4x'\mathcal{L}(x)-\int_{\Omega}d^4x\mathcal{L}(x)$$

From \(d^4x'=(1+\partial_{\mu}\delta x^{\mu})d^4x\), we have

$$\int_{\Omega'}d^4x'\delta\mathcal{L}(x)\approx \int_{\Omega}d^4x\delta\mathcal{L}(x)$$

Because \(\Omega'\) is just the same as \(\Omega\) and we omit the second and high order \(\delta\). Also:

$$\int_{\Omega'}d^4x'\mathcal{L}(x)-\int_{\Omega}d^4x\mathcal{L}(x)\\ =\int_{\Omega}d^4x\partial_{\mu}\delta x^{\mu}\mathcal{L}(x)$$

Then we have

$$\delta S=\int_{\Omega}d^4x\delta\mathcal{L}(x)+\int_{\Omega}d^4x\partial_{\mu}\delta x^{\mu}\mathcal{L}(x)\\ =\int_{\Omega}d^4x\left(\tilde{\delta}\mathcal{L}(x)+\partial_{\mu}\mathcal{L}(x)\delta x^{\mu}\right)+\int_{\Omega}d^4x\partial_{\mu}\delta x^{\mu}\mathcal{L}(x)\\ =\int_{\Omega}d^4x\left(\tilde{\delta}\mathcal{L}(x)+\partial_{\mu}(\delta x^{\mu}\mathcal{L}(x))\right)$$

Then we can get

$$\tilde{\delta}\mathcal{L}(x)=\partial^{\mu}\left[\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}(x)}{\partial(\partial^{\mu}\phi_r)}\tilde{\delta}\phi_r(x)\right],$$

where Euler-Lagrangian formula has been used. Define:

\begin{align} j_{\mu}(x)=&\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}(x)}{\partial(\partial^{\mu}\phi_r)}\tilde{\delta}\phi_r(x)+\mathcal{L}(x)\delta x_{\mu}\\ =&\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}(x)}{\partial(\partial^{\mu}\phi_r)}{\delta}\phi_r(x)-\left(\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}(x)}{\partial(\partial^{\mu}\phi_r)}\partial_{\nu}\phi_r(x)-g_{\mu\nu}\mathcal{L}(x)\right)\delta x^{\nu}, \end{align}

we get

$$\partial^{\mu}j_{\mu}(x)=0$$

Which is the Noether's theorem.

2. In the active point of view

What we want to do is to calculate the variantion

$$\delta S=\int_{\Omega'}d^4x'\mathcal{L}(\phi_r'(x'),\partial_{\mu}'\phi_r'(x'))-\int_{\Omega}d^4x\mathcal{L}(\phi_r(x),\partial_{\mu}\phi_r(x))$$

Since \(x'\) is a dumb variant, we can recall it as \(x\), then

\begin{align} \delta S=&\int_{\Omega'}d^4x\mathcal{L}(\phi_r'(x),\partial_{\mu}'\phi_r'(x))-\int_{\Omega}d^4x\mathcal{L}(\phi_r(x),\partial_{\mu}\phi_r(x))\\ =&\int_{\Omega}\tilde{\delta}\mathcal{L}(x)+\int_{\Omega'-\Omega}d^4x\mathcal{L}(\phi_r'(x),\partial_{\mu}'\phi_r'(x))\\ =&\int_{\Omega}\tilde{\delta}\mathcal{L}(x)+\int_{\partial\Omega}dS_{\lambda}\delta x^{\lambda}\mathcal{L}(\phi_r(x),\partial_{\mu}\phi_r(x))\\ =&\int_{\Omega}\partial_{\mu}(\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial(\partial_{\mu}\phi_r)}\tilde{\delta}\phi_r)+\int_{\Omega}dx^4\partial_{\lambda}(\delta x^{\lambda}\mathcal{L}(\phi_r(x),\partial_{\mu}\phi_r(x)))\\ =&\int_{\Omega}\partial_{\mu}(\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial(\partial_{\mu}\phi_r)}\tilde{\delta}\phi_r+\delta x^{\mu}\mathcal{L}) \end{align}

Where \(\tilde{\delta}\phi_r=\delta\phi_r-\partial_{\mu}\phi_r\delta x^{\mu}\).

3. Simpler one (akin to active point of view)

The coordinate is unchanged, but the field is changed to \(\phi(x')\) for scalar field and \({T(\Lambda)^{-1}}_r^s\phi_s(x')\) for vector field.

$$\delta\mathcal{L}=\partial_{\mu}(\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial(\partial_{\mu}\phi_r)}\delta\phi_r)=\mathcal{L}(x')-\mathcal{L}(x)$$

For translation symmetry, \(x'=x+\epsilon\), \(\delta\phi_r=\phi_r(x')-\phi_r(x)=\epsilon^{\mu}\partial_{\mu}\phi_r\), which gives

$$\partial_{\mu}(\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial(\partial^{\mu}\phi_r)}\partial_{\nu}\phi_r-g_{\mu\nu}\mathcal{L})=0$$

For Lorentz transformation, we have \(x'=x+\delta\omega^{\mu}{}_{\nu}x^{\nu}\),

$$\delta\phi_r=T(\Lambda)^{-1}{}_r{}^s\phi_s(x')-\phi(x)=\delta\omega_{\mu\nu}(x^{\nu}\partial^{\mu}\phi_r+\frac{i}{2}(J^{\mu\nu})_r{}^s\phi_s)$$

where \(T(\Lambda){}_r{}^s=\delta_r{}^s-\frac{i}{2}\delta_{\mu\nu}(J^{\mu\nu})_r{}^s\) we get

$$\partial^{\mu}f_{\mu}=0$$

with

$$f_{\mu}=\frac{1}{2}\delta\omega^{\nu\lambda}M_{\mu\nu\lambda}$$
$$M_{\mu\nu\lambda}=\Theta_{\mu\lambda}x_{\nu}-\Theta_{\mu\nu}x_{\lambda}+i\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial(\partial^{\mu}\phi_r)}(J_{\nu\lambda})_r{}^s\phi_s$$

where

$$\Theta_{\mu\nu}=\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial(\partial^{\mu}\phi_r)}\partial_{\nu}\phi_r-g_{\mu\nu}\mathcal{L}$$

Or another way around: \(\phi(x)\to T(\Lambda)\phi(\Lambda^{-1}x)\)
